AI Overview     
Single-cell organisms, such as bacteria, archaea, and algae, ingest phosphate primarily through active transport mechanisms across their plasma membrane to acquire inorganic phosphate (P{i}) from their surroundings. This process is essential for creating ATP, DNA, RNA, and phospholipids. [1-5]

Key methods of ingestion include:
- Active Transporters (Sodium/Phosphate Co-transporters): Microbes utilize specific transport proteins located in the plasma membrane, such as type II (SLC34) or type III (SLC20, often called Pit1/Pit2) transporters. These transporters use the energy derived from an electrochemical gradient—often sodium or proton gradients—to move phosphate from the environment into the cytoplasm.
- Phosphate-Specific Transport System (Pst): Many bacteria use a high-affinity Pst system to scavenge phosphorus when it is scarce in the environment, often regulated by the pho regulon.Enzymatic Release (Mineralization): When inorganic phosphate is scarce, some single-cell organisms secrete enzymes, such as phosphatase, to break down organic phosphorus compounds into soluble (P{i}) in the surrounding environment before taking it up.
- Storage (Polyphosphate): When phosphate is abundant, some microorganisms can take up more than they immediately need and store it within the cell as polyphosphate (polyP). [6-10]
Once inside the cell, this inorganic phosphate is immediately assimilated into ATP to meet the cell’s energetic needs. [11-13]

Single-cell organisms, such as bacteria, yeast, and microalgae, ingest phosphate primarily by absorbing inorganic phosphate Pi directly from their environment through active transport mechanisms located in their plasma [1,2].
Here is how they ingest and manage phosphate:
- Active Transport Systems: Because phosphate concentrations outside the cell are often low, single-cell organisms use energy to actively transport phosphate into the cell against a concentration gradient. Specialized phosphate transporters, such as Pit (low-affinity/high-capacity) and Pst (high-affinity/low-capacity) systems, are used by many bacteria.
- Signaling and Regulation: In unicellular organisms like bacteria and yeast, the uptake of inorganic phosphate (Pi) is controlled by specialized signaling pathways, such as the pho pathway, which sense external phosphate levels and regulate the expression of transporters accordingly.
- Enzymatic Release: When phosphorus is bound to organic molecules, organisms (such as microalgae and bacteria) secrete enzymes like phosphatases to cleave the organic molecules and release usable inorganic phosphate.
- Storage (Polyphosphate): When phosphate is abundant, many single-cell organisms absorb more than they need for immediate growth, storing it internally as long chains called polyphosphate (polyP).
- Phagocytosis: Some larger single-celled eukaryotic organisms, such as amoeba, can engulf food particles that contain phosphorus, which is then digested within a phagosome. [3-11]
Once inside the cell, this phosphate is utilized to generate ATP, nucleic acids (DNA/RNA), and phospholipids.[-1]
